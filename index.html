3D Data Formats

Common Formats


Every 3D file format stores information about the geometry of a 3-dimensional object. Each extension has its own characteristics, and some can store additional information, such as appearance information (colour, texture), animation, and the scene (camera position, lighting).


These are some of the most commonly used 3D file formats:

  • .STL (StereoLitography)
  • .OBJ (Object Wavefront)
  • .STEP (Standard for the Exchange Product Data)
  • .FBX (Filmbox)
  • .3DS (3D Studio)
  • .USD (Universal Scene Description)

Desired Outcome


Since each format has a different way of encoding and can store different aspects of a 3D shape, it is important to keep in mind the end result when working on a 3D model, to ensure that the extension we use will fit our needs accordingly. There are 4 main uses for 3D modeling. Hover above them to see the recommended file format to work in.

3D Printing
STL and OBJ are extensions compatible with most 3D modeling softwares and slicers, and the information they can hold is sufficient for the level of precision of today's 3D printers.

Engineering
STEP and IGES were created for CAD software, and they also support topology, textures, and other features relevant to engineering and computer-aided design.

Gaming
FBX and 3DS files were created with animation in mind. They support features such as skeletal animation and scene information, and are widely used in the video game industry.

Animation
FBX and the recent USD file format are becoming the industry standard when it comes to animation. They both store information on the geometry, colour, texture, animation, scene and lighting. USD files even have a collaborative feature, which allow you to work with other people on the same project.
